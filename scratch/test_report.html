TL;DR
Under a strict closed-loop self-evolution formulation where only an unlabeled prompt set and a base model are available, this paper systematically compares increasingly structured self-evolution strategies (single-round verification, multi-turn revision, iterative training, curriculum learning) against oracle-supervised preference optimization. On Knights and Knaves logical reasoning tasks, self-evolution consistently improves over the base model but plateaus and leaves a non-trivial generalization gap to oracle supervision, with the exception of multi-turn revision at 12B scale nearly matching oracle performance. On real-world reasoning benchmarks, gains are modest and diminishing, highlighting that internally generated supervision alone remains insufficient to replace verifiable rewards or curated ground-truth solutions.

Benchmarks

Knights and Knaves (KK) logical reasoning benchmark (Xie et al., 2024), OpenThoughts3 (Guha et al., 2025) for mathematical reasoning training, GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), MATH500 subset of MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021), MATHHard (difficulty level 5 of MATH), TabMWP (Lu et al., 2022)

Previous Work & Limitations

The paper situates itself in the context of post-training techniques for improving LLM reasoning, including Supervised Finetuning (SFT), Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), and Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards (RLVR). Many recent self-evolution methods leverage internal model signals: R-Zero uses online RL without external data; INTUITOR employs self-certainty as reward; LSP and EMPO use intrinsic model-based rewards; Absolute Zero (AZR) builds an online self-play RL pipeline with code execution, but still relies on an external executor. Prior work on self-verification includes Piterbarg et al., Shafayat et al., Zeng et al., and Zhang et al.. Studies like Song et al. and Sun et al. highlight the generator-verifier gap; Zhang et al. (2025c) show that rejection-sampling pipelines are insufficient for sustained self-improvement. Risks of learning from synthetic data such as model collapse (Dey & Donoho, Shumailov et al.) and theoretical barriers further motivate the need to rigorously measure the gap between closed-loop self-evolution and oracle supervision. Orthogonal test-time scaling methods (Lightman et al., Zhang et al.) and self-refinement techniques (Madaan et al., Kumar et al.) complement this work but differ by focusing on inference-time or online improvements rather than offline parameter updates under a strict closed-loop constraint.

Related Work Landscape

This paper is highly relevant to generator-verifier dynamics and closed-loop training. It is closely related to SPIN (Self-Play Fine-Tuning) and AZR (Absolute Zero).

Method

The paper instantiates a unified offline generator–verifier (GV) game where a single base model $\mathcal{M}$ acts as both generator $\mathcal{G}$ and verifier $\mathcal{V}$ via different system prompts. Given an unlabeled prompt set $\mathcal{D}$, the generator produces $k$ candidate solutions $\{\hat{y}_1,\dots,\hat{y}_k\}$ per query $q$, and the verifier outputs binary judgments $\mathcal{V}(q,\hat{y}_i) \in \{\text{Correct},\text{Incorrect}\}$. Preference pairs $(y_w,y_l)$ are extracted when $\mathcal{V}$ labels one response correct and another incorrect. To reduce noise, thresholded majority voting is applied: the verifier is queried $n$ times per candidate, an empirical correctness rate $\hat{p}$ is computed, and a candidate is labeled positive if $\hat{p} \ge \tau$, negative if $1-\hat{p} \ge \tau$, and discarded otherwise (with $\tau \in [0.5,0.8]$). The resulting preference data is used to fine-tune with Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) by minimizing $\mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}}(\pi_\theta;\pi_{\text{ref}}) = -\mathbb{E}\big[\log\sigma(\beta\log\frac{\pi_\theta(y_w|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w|x)} - \beta\log\frac{\pi_\theta(y_l|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l|x)})\big]$.

Four self-evolution strategies are compared: - SimpleSE (single-round): direct verification without feedback ($T=1$). - RevisionSE (multi-turn): the verifier provides textual feedback, and the generator refines output iteratively; a preference pair is formed iff $\mathcal{V}$ rejects an initial response and accepts a later revision ($\hat{y}^{(t)}\text{ incorrect} \to \hat{y}^{(t+1)}\text{ correct}$). - Iterative SE: repeatedly apply the GV game and DPO over rounds, using the same prompt set $\mathcal{D}$: $\mathcal{P}_t = \text{GV}(\mathcal{M}_{t-1},\mathcal{D},T)$, $\mathcal{M}_t = \text{Finetune}(\mathcal{M}_{t-1},\mathcal{P}_t)$. - Curriculum SE: partition prompts by difficulty (e.g., number of people in KK), train on easy splits first before progressing to harder ones.

For all configurations, an oracle verifier that uses ground-truth answers and exact-match is also evaluated to quantify the upper bound. The framework is offline; all preference data is generated once per round, and models are instruction-tuned variants (Gemma-3-it, Qwen-2.5-Instruct) to enable the dual role.

Performance Evaluation

Knights and Knaves (KK) results: - Gemma 3 4B: base accuracy 31.0%. SimpleSE $\tau=0.6$: 40.7%; RevisionSE: 42.2%; Iterative SE (3 rounds): 44.1%; Curriculum SE: 44.8%. Oracle verifier after single round: 46.6%; with additional oracle round after SE iterations: 53.2–53.3%. Thus, a persistent gap of ~8–13% to oracle supervision remains. - Gemma 3 12B: base 47.5%. SimpleSE $\tau=0.6$: 51.1%; RevisionSE: 52.8%, nearly matching oracle verifier at 53.6% (only ~0.8% gap). Oracle verifier after iterative SE: 52.6–53.3%. - Qwen 2.5 7B: base 17.3%, SimpleSE 19.4%, RevisionSE 19.4%, Oracle 30.6%, showing similar trends. - Curriculum learning consistently outperforms random mixing of difficulty levels, both for self-evolution and oracle settings. - Cost analysis: scaling verifier passes ($n_2$) is more impact-per-token than scaling generator samples ($n_1$); threshold $\tau=0.7$ strikes the best precision–recall balance (e.g., 41.7% for 4B).

General reasoning benchmarks (OpenThoughts3 training): - Gemma 3 4B: on GSM8K 89.2% → 89.0% (negligible); MATH500 75.8% → 77.4%; MATHHard 53.7% → 55.1%; TabMWP 84.5% → 87.4%; KK 31.0% → 33.2%. Improvements are modest compared to KK gains, especially for open-ended tasks. - Gemma 3 12B and Qwen2.5-7B show similar small gains, e.g., Qwen 7B MATH500 73.5% → 76.0%. - Comparison with online self-evolution baselines (INTUITOR, AZR): SimpleSE (offline) achieves competitive or better performance without external executors, e.g., Gemma 4B beats INTUITOR and AZR on several benchmarks. - Scaling data size (5k–40k preference pairs) shows diminishing returns; performance plateaus or slightly degrades for TabMWP and KK beyond 20k. - Pass@1 vs. Pass@32 analysis: SimpleSE improves Pass@1 (e.g., 31.0% → 40.7%) but Pass@32 remains nearly unchanged (~78%), indicating sharpening of existing solution paths rather than discovering new capabilities. - Verifier recall bottleneck: Gemma 4B recall improves from 52% to 62% after one SE round, while Gemma 1B recall remains low (36%), explaining limited improvement for smaller models. - RevisionSE step analysis: successful corrections typically require 3–5 revision steps; however, the process also introduces noise (e.g., 12% of initially correct solutions become incorrect after one revision for 4B), limiting gains for weaker models.

Critical Analysis

The paper presents a thorough empirical investigation of closed-loop self-evolution, but several methodological and reporting issues weaken its conclusions. The comparisons to online methods are not controlled, statistical significance is not reported, and the analysis of bottlenecks relies on limited data. Generalizability to other models and tasks remains unverified.

  • [MEDIUM] The comparison with online self-evolution methods (INTUITOR, AZR) is not controlled; models differ in base architecture, training regime, and access to external tools. Claims that SimpleSE outperforms these methods may be misleading without direct, fair comparisons.

  • [MEDIUM] The paper does not report statistical significance tests for key comparisons (e.g., RevisionSE vs. oracle, SimpleSE vs. base model), which weakens the reliability of claimed performance gaps and the conclusion of near-oracle performance.

  • [MEDIUM] The verifier accuracy on MATH is much higher than on KK (≈85% vs. 60% for Gemma 4B), yet self-evolution gains are much smaller on MATH. This discrepancy is not sufficiently discussed, limiting the generalizability of the findings.

  • [LOW] The evidence that verifier recall is a bottleneck relies on a small sample of 100 examples (Appendix A.2); conclusions drawn from this sample may not be robust.

  • [MEDIUM] The experiments use only two model families (Gemma and Qwen) and only instruction-tuned variants; generalizability to other LLMs, base models, or different architectures remains unverified.

  • [LOW] The paper focuses exclusively on DPO for preference learning; other alignment algorithms (e.g., PPO, KTO) might interact differently with self-generated data, but are not explored.

  • [LOW] The iterative SE setting reuses the same prompt set across rounds without reporting training-set performance or overfitting analysis, which could mask overfitting issues.

  • [LOW] Curriculum learning evaluation only considers a simple two-stage easy-to-hard curriculum on KK; more nuanced curricula or curricula on other tasks are not studied, limiting the generality of the claim that curriculum helps SE.

  • [LOW] The cost analysis in Figure 3 reports average accuracy across all thresholds, which may obscure performance at optimal thresholds; the reported trade-offs might not directly translate to practical settings where one would choose the best threshold.
